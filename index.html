⚠️ Common Cyber Threats

Cyber threats are malicious acts that seek to damage data, steal data, or disrupt digital life.

  • Malware: Software designed to harm devices or networks
  • Phishing: Fake emails/messages tricking users to reveal information
  • Identity Theft: Using someone's personal information without permission
  • Cyberbullying: Harassment through digital platforms
  • DDoS Attacks: Overwhelming systems to make them unavailable

🛡️ Cybersecurity Safeguards

Protect yourself with these essential cybersecurity practices:

  • Use strong, unique passwords for each account
  • Enable two-factor authentication when available
  • Be suspicious of unsolicited messages and links
  • Keep software and apps updated
  • Adjust privacy settings on social media
  • Think before you post personal information

👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 Protecting Children Online

Children are especially vulnerable to online threats. Here's how to protect them:

  • Monitor their online activities and set time limits
  • Teach them about privacy and not sharing personal information
  • Use parental controls and safe search options
  • Encourage open communication about their online experiences
  • Educate them about cyberbullying and how to respond

Page 1: Understanding Cybersecurity: Threats and Safeguards

What is Cybersecurity?

Cybersecurity is the practice of protecting systems, networks, programs, and data from digital attacks. Think of it as the digital equivalent of locking your doors, not sharing your keys, and being aware of your surroundings to avoid pickpockets.

Common Cyber Threats (The "Bad Guys"):

Malware: Malicious software designed to harm your device.

  • Viruses/Worms: Spread and damage files or systems.
  • Ransomware: Locks your files and demands money to unlock them.
  • Spyware: Secretly records what you do on your device.

Phishing:

Fake emails, texts, or messages that trick you into revealing personal information like passwords or bank details. They often look like they're from a trusted source (e.g., your bank, a popular app).

Identity Theft:

When a criminal uses your personal information (like your ID number or name) to commit fraud, such as opening accounts or taking out loans in your name.

Cyberbullying:

Using digital technology to harass, threaten, or embarrass someone. This can happen on social media, in games, or through messaging.

Privacy Violations:

When your personal information (photos, location, messages) is collected or shared without your permission.

Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) Attacks:

Overwhelming a website or online service with fake traffic from many sources, making it crash and become unavailable to real users.

Basic Cybersecurity Safeguards (The "Good Habits"):

  • Use Strong Passwords: Combine letters (upper and lower case), numbers, and symbols. Use a different password for each important account.
  • Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA): This adds a second step (like a code sent to your phone) to log in, making it much harder for hackers to get in.
  • Think Before You Click: Don't click on links or download attachments in suspicious emails or messages.
  • Update Software: Always install updates for your apps and operating system. These updates often fix security holes.
  • Be Careful on Public Wi-Fi: Avoid doing sensitive things like online banking on public networks. Use a VPN if possible.
  • Check Privacy Settings: Regularly review the privacy settings on your social media accounts to control who sees your information.

Page 2: Protecting Children in the Digital World

Children are especially vulnerable online. Here's how to protect them from specific threats:

1. Protecting from Identity Theft

A child's clean credit history is a valuable target for thieves.

Safeguards:

  • Keep Documents Safe: Don't casually share your child's birth certificate or ID number online.
  • Teach Secrecy: Explain that their full name, address, and ID number are private and should not be shared in games or on social profiles.
  • Ask Questions: Be wary of forms from schools or clubs asking for sensitive data—ask why they need it and how it will be protected.

2. Spotting and Stopping Phishing

Safeguards:

  • Look for Red Flags: Urgent language, spelling mistakes, generic greetings ("Dear User"), and suspicious sender addresses.
  • Hover, Don't Click: Hover your mouse over a link to see the real web address before clicking.
  • Verify Separately: If a message claims to be from a friend or company, contact them through a different method (e.g., a phone call) to confirm.

3. Preventing and Responding to Cyberbullying

Safeguards:

  • Don't Respond: Replying often makes the bullying worse.
  • Save the Evidence: Take screenshots of the messages, posts, or pictures.
  • Block and Report: Use platform tools to block the bully and report their behavior.
  • Tell a Trusted Adult: Encourage children to talk to a parent, teacher, or counselor immediately.

4. Guarding Privacy

Safeguards:

  • "Think Before You Post": Once something is online, it's almost impossible to completely remove it.
  • Disable Location Tags: Turn off geotagging on photos posted to social media.
  • Use "Friends Only" Settings: Limit who can see posts and personal information.

Page 4: Cybersecurity Risks Specific to Maseru High School Students

Based on the digital profile, here are the most likely and dangerous threats facing these students:

Social Media Phishing & Scams:

  • Risk: Fake login pages for Instagram or Facebook sent via DM, offering "free vouchers" or claiming an account violation. Students might enter their credentials, handing them directly to a scammer.
  • Consequence: Hacked social accounts used for further scams or cyberbullying.

Cyberbullying and Online Harassment:

  • Risk: The anonymity of online platforms can lead to hurtful comments, spreading of rumors, or creation of fake profiles to harass peers.
  • Consequence: Severe emotional distress, anxiety, depression, and impact on academic performance.

Privacy Violations and "Doxing":

  • Risk: Oversharing personal information, location check-ins, or personal photos. In a conflict, this information can be weaponized and shared publicly without consent ("doxing").
  • Consequence: Real-world stalking, embarrassment, and personal safety risks.

Gaming-Related Risks:

  • Risk: Scams offering "free" in-game currency, phishing links in game chats, or cyberbullying within multiplayer games.
  • Consequence: Loss of game accounts, malware infection, or exposure to toxic behavior.

DDoS Attacks on School Infrastructure:

  • Risk: As noted, DDoS attacks are common in education. A student might use a cheap, readily available online service to attack the school's network, perhaps to avoid an online test or as a prank.
  • Consequence: The school's website, learning portal, or internet access crashes, disrupting education for everyone.

Page 5: An Interactive Solution for Maseru High School

"CyberSmart Maseru" - A Cybersecurity Awareness Program

This is a proposed interactive solution to teach students about safe behaviors and risks.

Core Components:

Short, Engaging Video Modules:

  • Create 3-minute animated videos addressing specific topics: "How to Spot a Fake WhatsApp Message," "What to Do If You're Being Cyberbullied," "Why Your Password is Like Your Toothbrush."

Interactive Quiz Game ("Phish or Real?"):

  • A game built into the school's learning portal or as a simple app. Students are shown examples of messages, emails, and social media posts and must correctly identify them as "Phish" or "Real." Leaderboards can create healthy competition.

Student "Cyber Ambassador" Program:

  • Train a group of interested students to become peer mentors. They can lead workshops, create content, and be the first point of contact for students facing online issues.

Simulated Phishing Exercises:

  • With the school's permission, send fake (but harmless) phishing emails to students. Those who click get redirected to a short training video instead of a malicious site. This is a powerful, hands-on lesson.

What It Teaches:

  • Safe Behaviors: Creating strong passwords, using privacy settings, thinking before posting.
  • Disparate Risks: Understanding that threats can come from games, social media, and emails.
  • Identifying Phishing: Learning the red flags through practice in a safe environment.
  • How to Deal with Threats: Clear steps for reporting cyberbullying, phishing, and other incidents to a teacher or ambassador.

Conclusion: The Path Forward

Cybersecurity is not just a technical issue; it is a community and educational one. For Maseru High School students, the digital world offers incredible opportunities for learning and connection, but it also presents real dangers.

The key to safety lies in proactive education. Page 7: Advanced Cybersecurity Concepts

Network Security Fundamentals

Understanding how networks work and how to secure them is crucial in today's interconnected world.

Key Concepts:

  • Firewalls: Security systems that control incoming and outgoing network traffic
  • VPNs (Virtual Private Networks): Encrypted connections that provide privacy and security
  • Encryption: Converting information into a code to prevent unauthorized access
  • Two-Factor Authentication: Adding an extra layer of security beyond just a password

Social Engineering Awareness

Social engineering is the psychological manipulation of people to divulge confidential information or perform actions.

Common Techniques:

  • Pretexting: Creating a fabricated scenario to engage a target
  • Baiting: Offering something enticing to lure victims
  • Quid Pro Quo: Requesting personal information in exchange for a service
  • Tailgating: Following someone into a restricted area without authorization

Incident Response Planning

Having a plan for when security incidents occur is just as important as prevention.

Key Steps:

  1. Preparation: Establish policies and procedures
  2. Identification: Detect and analyze potential security incidents
  3. Containment: Limit the damage and isolate affected systems
  4. Eradication: Remove the cause of the incident
  5. Recovery: Restore systems and operations
  6. Lessons Learned: Document and improve based on the experience

Phishing Simulation Exercises

In a controlled environment, we send simulated phishing emails to help students practice identifying red flags. Those who click on suspicious links are redirected to educational content instead of malicious sites.

How It Works

  1. Students receive simulated phishing emails
  2. Those who identify them as phishing can report them
  3. Students who click links are redirected to training
  4. No personal data is collected or compromised
  5. The goal is education, not punishment

What You'll Learn

  • Recognize phishing email characteristics
  • Identify suspicious links and attachments
  • Understand social engineering tactics
  • Practice safe email habits
  • Know how to report suspicious messages
